Interesting Places in Canterbury

Canterbury Museum

Canterbury Museum, situated in the heart of Christchurch's Cultural Precinct, is a cornerstone of the city's heritage, offering a captivating journey through the natural and human history of Canterbury, New Zealand, and beyond. Established in 1867 with the extensive collections of its first director, Julius von Haast, the museum's Gothic Revival building, designed by Benjamin Mountfort and continually expanded since its opening in 1870, is itself a significant architectural landmark. The museum boasts a vast and diverse collection, ranging from comprehensive displays on New Zealand's unique natural history, including its iconic moa, to a renowned Hall of Antarctic Discovery, commemorating the heroic age of polar exploration. Its exhibitions also delve into the rich cultural heritage of Māori, the story of early European settlement in Canterbury, and an eclectic array of items from around the world, making it a beloved institution that continues to educate and inspire. The museum is currently undergoing a significant redevelopment, with a pop-up museum at a different location showcasing highlights from its vast collection during this time.

Canterbury Provincial Council Buildings

The Provincial Chambers in Christchurch are an exceptionally significant piece of New Zealand's architectural and political heritage, standing as the only purpose-built provincial government buildings still in existence in the country. Designed by the eminent Gothic Revival architect Benjamin Mountfort, the complex was constructed in three distinct stages between 1858 and 1865, each reflecting the growing prosperity of the Canterbury Province during its self-governing period. Its most celebrated feature was the Stone Chamber, renowned as one of the finest High Victorian Gothic interiors outside England, characterized by its intricate detailing, use of polychromy (coloured stonework), and a grand, richly decorated ceiling. Following the abolition of provincial governments in 1876, the buildings served various governmental departments, and a portion was even returned to local control in 1928, marking a pioneering instance of heritage protection legislation in New Zealand. Sadly, the Provincial Chambers suffered severe damage in the 2011 Christchurch earthquake, particularly the Stone Chamber, which saw its roof collapse, and the buildings have remained largely closed to the public since, undergoing stabilization and extensive assessment while discussions continue regarding their complex and costly restoration.

The Arts Centre

The Arts Centre (Te Matatiki Toi Ora) lies at the heart of Christchurch City, New Zealand.The precinct is a cultural heritage site which features beautiful architecture in the Gothic Revival style, which is characteristic of heritage buildings in Christchurch. The construction of the buildings started in 1877 and were originally built as the campus for Canterbury College (Now known as Canterbury University) . This is what the buildings were used for until the University announced their decision to move to their campus Ilam in 1949. Today, The Arts Centre has become a tourist hub, it is free to enter and there are a range of activities that you can do there. The centre also hosts a diverse program of events, performances, and markets, making it an essential heart of Christchurch's artistic and cultural scene. Visitors can explore independent retailers, artisan workshops, galleries, museums, and eateries, all set amidst beautiful courtyards and historic architecture. The Arts Centre faced significant damage in the 2010/2011 Christchurch Earthquake. After a lengthy restoration process which took 11 years to complete it has re-emerged as a vibrant hub for creativity, innovation, and heritage.

Ferrymead Heritage Park

Ferrymead Heritage Park, nestled in the Heathcote Valley of Christchurch, is a sprawling outdoor museum that offers a vivid journey back in time, primarily to an Edwardian-era township from the early 1900s. Established in 1964 by a collection of local heritage enthusiasts, it occupies the historically significant site of New Zealand's first public railway, which opened in 1863. The park features a meticulously recreated village, comprising a mix of authentic relocated buildings and faithful replicas, including a church, schoolhouse, bakery, jail, and various period-style businesses. Beyond the village, Ferrymead is a living showcase of early 20th-century technology and industry, housing an impressive array of heritage exhibitions maintained by passionate volunteer societies, ranging from operational steam trains and trams to vintage fire engines, aircraft, printing presses, and telecommunications equipment, providing an immersive and interactive experience of Christchurch's rich past.

Riccarton House & Bush

Riccarton House and Bush (Pūtaringamotu) is a unique heritage site in Christchurch, New Zealand, preserving a significant piece of both natural and cultural history. The grand Riccarton House was built in three stages between 1856 and 1900. The pioneering Deans family built the homestead. There the first permanent European settlers on the Canterbury Plains. Gifted to the people of Christchurch by the Deans family in 1914 and administered by the Riccarton Bush Trust, this site offers visitors a tranquil escape into a protected native forest, home to diverse flora and fauna, alongside the opportunity to explore the restored historic house and learn about the compelling story of the Deans family and early European settlement. Adjacent to the house lies Riccarton Bush, a rare 7.8-hectare remnant of ancient kahikatea floodplain forest, with some trees up to 600 years old, representing the only surviving example of this once-widespread ecosystem in Canterbury. This bush is a haven for native flora and fauna. The Deans bush walk features native tree species such as ancient kahikatea trees, some of which have graced the landscape for up to 600 years. Other native trees such as matai, tōtara, hīnau, tītoki, pōkākā, and kōwhai can also be seen on the bush walk. This rich bush in the city provides a small oasis for bird species such as fantail (pīwakawaka), the industrious silvereye (tauhou), and the discreet grey warbler (riroriro). The impressive wood pigeon (kererū) finds a crucial habitat here, forming a significant part of Christchurch's resident population. Notably, the bush also plays a vital role in the conservation of the great-spotted kiwi/roroa, serving as a crèche where young birds are raised before their release into the wild.

Canterbury Earthquake National Memorial

The Canterbury Earthquake National Memorial (Oi Manawa) is a solemn tribute to the lives lost and the resilience of the Christchurch community following the devastating earthquakes of 2010 and 2011. Opened on 22 February 2017, the sixth anniversary of the 6.3 magnitude earthquake, the memorial is located along the Ōtākaro/Avon River, near the Montreal Street bridge. Designed by Slovenian architect Grega Vezjak, it features a 111-metre-long curved wall inscribed with the names of all 185 victims, written in both English and their native languages. The memorial serves as a place of reflection, acknowledging not only those who perished but also the survivors, first responders, and the collective trauma endured by the city. A pounamu (greenstone) gifted by Te Rūnanga o Makaawhio marks the entrance, symbolizing connection to the land and those who came before. This space stands as a poignant reminder of the strength and unity that emerged from tragedy.

Kaiapoi Monument

The Kaiapoi Monument, located just north of the present-day town of Kaiapoi, stands as a poignant memorial to the historic Kaiapoi Pā (Te Kōhaka-a-Kaikaiāwaro), the formidable Ngāi Tahu stronghold that dominated the area for centuries. Erected in 1898 largely through the efforts of Canon James Stack, the monument commemorates the lives of those who lived and died at the pā, particularly those lost during the devastating siege and sacking by Ngāti Toa warriors led by Te Rauparaha in 1831-32. Designed by renowned architect Samuel Hurst Seager and featuring intricate stone carving by Charles Kidson, the monument itself is a significant piece of New Zealand architectural and sculptural heritage. Though the original tekoteko (carved figure) that once topped the column was removed after the 2011 Canterbury earthquakes and is currently on display at the Quake City exhibition in Christchurch, the monument remains a powerful symbol of cultural identity and historic continuity, marking a site considered sacred to Ngāi Tahu as "the nest or cradle of the tribe."
